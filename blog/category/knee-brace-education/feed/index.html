It’s evident that hiking is strenuous on the knees. The uneven terrain, rocky surfaces, and uphill climbs can affect your joints, muscles, and ligaments. If you’re an avid hiker or adventure seeker who experiences knee pain while on the trail, you may wonder if it’s best to wear a knee brace while you hike to ease the stress on your body. In this article, we will walk you through the benefits of wearing a knee brace while hiking, who they are right for, and help you find the most suitable solution.

Is Knee Pain Common While Hiking?

Short answer, yes. One study suggested that 50% of hiking injuries were sprained and strained knees, ankles, and back muscles. Even if you’re active, healthy, and spending every weekend on the trail, you are still susceptible to knee pain or injury, especially considering our knee joints carry a large majority of our body weight. When you’re walking on a hike filled with inclines and uneven terrain, it can lead to the development of knee pain. If left untreated, this can lead to injuries in the future.

Who Should Wear a Knee Brace While Hiking?

Knee braces are designed for anyone who wants to prevent or treat pain or injuries while hiking or performing other activities. Pre-existing pain is not a prerequisite to using a knee brace or support. Quality knee braces such as an unloader knee brace are designed to redistribute weight and pressure, providing preventative support to anyone who is frequently active or consistently spending a long time on their feet. If you are unsure as to whether a knee brace is right for you, speak with your doctor.

Benefits of Wearing a Knee Brace While Hiking

There are many benefits that wearing a knee brace can have. Some of these benefits may be more useful to some than others, but it is good to understand them all.

Protects the Knees from Injury

A knee brace for hiking can be a preventative measure that helps combat knee pain and shift pressure away from your joints while you hike uphill or downhill. With the right knee brace, you’ll gain peace of mind knowing that your knees are supported every step of the way. Allowing you to enjoy yourself while on and off the trail.

Improves Overall Stability

Research suggests that wearing a knee brace can provide unstable, injured knees with additional support. This is especially true for individuals that suffer from an anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) injury. With the right knee brace, you can confidently walk on uneven terrain and gain the confidence you need while hiking, so you don’t need to give up your favorite hobbies.

Enhances Performance

If you’re a serious hiker, you want to have the ability to control your movements and not be limited by your injuries. Greater mobility allows you to climb steeper inclines and take on the most demanding hikes. With the right hiking knee brace, you’ll receive the boost you need to tackle these challenges and confidently push yourself out of your comfort zone without risking injury.

Provides Additional Support

Knee braces are designed to give your knee the extra support they need. When you’re hiking downhill, this puts additional stress on your knees and can lead to discomfort. When you’re walking uphill, the cartilage under the kneecap, called chondromalacia patellae, is susceptible to softening and is prone to damage. Rocky or uneven terrain can also lead to pain and discomfort due to missed steps or falls. With the right knee brace, you can receive the additional support you need to conquer the hiking trails with ease.

Increases Comfort

Whether you’re going downhill or uphill – You’re putting a lot of pressure on your knees and the muscles supporting the knee. This isn’t comfortable and can lead to injury down the line. If you aren’t wearing a knee brace, joints, and ligaments can be weakened and may buckle while you’re on the trail.

With a proper knee brace, you can protect yourself from falling or hurting your knees. Even if an injury isn’t at the top of your list of concerns, the comfort, and peace of mind a proper knee brace brings can enhance the experience of any hike.

What Are The Best Knee Supports for Hiking?

When it comes to finding the best knee support, it’s not as straightforward as one might think. Each type of knee brace provides a different level of support for your knees. Knowing the details is crucial to making the best decision for yourself.

Here are three different options to consider.

I. Patella Knee Strap

A knee strap is a lightweight, easy-to-wear strap that wraps below your kneecap. It’s designed to elevate some of the pressure associated with hiking. These light-support knee straps could work for individuals who experience minor pain or discomfort in their lower kneecaps. While a knee strap can be helpful, it lacks the structure and support needed for more seasoned hikers and may fail to adequately protect the knees in certain cases.

II. Knee Sleeves

A knee sleeve is a wearable, lightweight option that won’t get in the way while hiking. They are typically made with nylon or spandex material, making them easy to slip on and off. The downside is most knee sleeves provide very minimal support. If you’re experiencing ongoing pain, you’ll want to speak to your medical provider to find a suitable treatment plan and knee support.

III. Unloader Knee Braces

Unloader knee braces are medical-grade knee braces that help redistribute the weight and take pressure off parts of your knees. This minimizes friction and gives you enhanced support and stability to confidently take on hikes of every intensity. These are the top choice for injured athletes, injured persons, and hikers alike.

There are two types of unloader knee braces to consider: multi-compartmental and uni-compartmental. Multi-compartmental knee braces are designed to shift the weight from all parts of the knee. These are recommended for individuals suffering a serious injury or who are susceptible to injury.

While uni-compartmental only shifts the weight from one compartment of the knee. Both are designed to provide next-level protection and comfort while on the trail. If you need help determining which option suits you and your concerns, talk with your doctor.

Should I Hike with a Knee Brace?

Whether you’re an experienced hiker or looking to hike more frequently, you’ll want a high-quality knee brace to keep your joints supported and protect your knees. Without a knee brace, you can be at risk for serious injury or re-injury. Out of all the options available, a high-quality and durable knee brace is ideal.

It’s custom-fit to improve comfort and stability and give you the confidence to hike and perform strenuous exercises without concern.

What is a Meniscus Tear?

A meniscus tear is one of the most common knee injuries, affecting approximately 1 million people in the U.S. each year, regardless of age or fitness level. If you’ve suffered a meniscus tear or injury, your physician may advise wearing a knee brace as part of your treatment plan. The best knee brace for a meniscus tear will offer both support and protection for the meniscus, safeguarding it against any excessive force or pressure that could potentially worsen the injury. 

A meniscus tear is an injury to the cartilage in the knee joint that provides cushioning and stability to the knee. A meniscus tear can occur in several ways, including twisting the knee with excessive force or overextending it. The symptoms of a meniscus tear may include severe knee pain, inflammation, and difficulty in bending the knee. As with any knee injury, the treatment plan can vary from person to person. The recovery journey, depending on the tear’s magnitude, may involve physical therapy, pain management medication, and in severe cases, surgical intervention.

There are two types of meniscus tears.

Acute Tears

Acute knee tears are frequently caused by sudden trauma during physical activity. When the knee is over-extended or twisted too quickly, an acute tear can occur. Oftentime acute tears happen in conjunction with other injuries, particularly anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) tears. The extent of the tear will dictate the treatment required, with many acute tears requiring surgical intervention.

Degenerative Tears

Degenerative tears, in contrast to acute tears, deteriorate and intensify over time. This type of tear is a result of the gradual wear and tear of the hyaline cartilage and meniscus, which can take several years to manifest as knee pain. Unfortunately, most degenerative meniscus tears are not diagnosed until the damage has reached a point of significant discomfort. Unlike acute tears, surgery is not always necessary for degenerative tears.

Types of Knee Braces for a Meniscus Tear

Depending on the injury, surgery might be necessary in order to repair the meniscus or remove the torn portion. In either case, it is likely that your doctor will recommend a supportive knee brace to protect and stabilize the injured meniscus depending on the extent of your injury.

The type of knee brace you should wear for a meniscus tear depends on the extent of your injury and where you are in the recovery process. Listed below are some common types of knee braces for a meniscus tear:

Stabilizing Knee Brace

A stabilizing knee brace provides substantial stability and support to the knee and is often recommended for the early stages of recovery after a meniscus tear.

Compression Knee Sleeve

A compression knee sleeve is designed to provide compression to the knee in order to reduce swelling and pain. Compression knee sleeves are a great option for individuals looking to still stay active and engaged in physical activity while wearing a brace.

Hinged Knee Brace

A hinged knee brace provides additional support and stability to the knee and may be recommended for moderate to severe meniscus tears.

Unloader Knee Brace

An unloader brace is another great option for patients suffering from a meniscus tear, especially for those who also have osteoarthritis in addition to their meniscus injury. An unloader brace is designed to redistribute the weight bearing load away from the impacted area of the knee and onto the brace frame, which can help to reduce pain and increase mobility.

As for any other injury or illness, it is important to consult with a doctor or physical therapist to determine the best form of treatment for you.

The medial tibiofemoral (TF) compartment is the part of the knee joint that sits on the inner side of the knee, between the thigh bone (femur) and the shin bone (tibia). This compartment includes the medial meniscus and the medial collateral ligament (MCL).

The medial meniscus is a crescent-shaped piece of cartilage that acts as a shock absorber and helps to distribute weight across the joint. The MCL connects the thigh bone to the shin bone inside the knee and is critical for stability and proper alignment.

Lateral vs. Medial Unloader Knee Brace

One of the major decisions you will have to make is selecting a lateral or a medial unloader knee brace. To understand the difference, we will take a closer look at the anatomy of your knee and how each brace supports it. The main difference between lateral and medial unloader knee braces lies in which ligament they support. A lateral unloader knee brace supports the lateral collateral ligament (LCL). This ligament runs outside your knee, connecting the femur, or thigh bone, with the fibula, or calf bone. The LCL helps you resist impact from the inner surface of the knee. It is also responsible for preventing your knee from collapsing outward.

Conversely, a medial unloading brace supports your medial collateral ligament (MCL). This band runs inside your knee and connects your thigh bone with the tibia or shin bone. Its primary function is preventing your knee from collapsing inwards. Both ligaments control any sideways motion you take with your knees and stop or prevent unusual movements that could hurt your joints.

Lateral unloader knee braces are also known as lateral stabilizer knee braces or lateral patella stabilizer braces. Their name hints at some of its most relevant functions. Functions of Medial Unloader Knee Braces

Medial knee braces are also known as medial compartment knee braces. They support the medial compartment on the inside of your knee joint. These braces improve knee movement by preventing even partial joint collapse toward the inside and promoting healthier movement.
